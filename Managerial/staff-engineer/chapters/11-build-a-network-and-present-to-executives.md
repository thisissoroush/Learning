# Chapter 11: Build a Network of Peers & Present to Executives
## *Two Essential Skills for Long-Term Senior Impact*

---

## Part 1: Build a Network of Peers

### 🎯 Core Concept

The most consistent recommendation from Staff+ engineers: build a personal network of peers doing similar work. Not a huge network — a trusted, small, authentic one.

> *"It's those conversations about career challenges and growth that have gotten me to where I am in my career."* — Ritu Vincent

> *"Just like management, it gets lonely the higher up you go, and it's important to find peers that will still challenge you."* — Keavy McMinn

---

### 🌐 Three Levels of Network Building

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                         NETWORK BUILDING APPROACHES                      │
│                                                                          │
│  AMBIENT NETWORK           INTERNAL NETWORK        PERSONAL NETWORK     │
│  ───────────────           ────────────────        ────────────────     │
│  Following industry         Building connections    Deep relationships   │
│  leaders on Twitter         within your company     with specific peers  │
│                                                                          │
│  Low effort                 Medium effort           High effort          │
│  Low intimacy               Medium intimacy         High intimacy        │
│  Good starting point        Directly improves       Most impactful       │
│                             daily work              long-term            │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### How to Start (For Introverts)

The simplest approach that works:
1. Find someone you respect
2. Send a **short 1-2 paragraph email** with a specific question asking for advice
3. If they reply, thank them and send another question in 6-12 months
4. If they ask you for help, do what you can
5. If they don't reply: no worries, move on

> The worst outcome — they don't reply — is completely fine.

### Be Visible to Attract Your Network

The easiest way to build a network is being *findable* as a Staff+ engineer:
- **Write publicly** about Staff+ engineering (blog posts, articles)
- **Speak at tech conferences** — speaker networks become job networks
- **Join Slack communities** like the Rands Leadership Slack (`#staff-principal-engineering`)
- **Build internally** — current coworkers will eventually spread across the industry

### Quality Over Quantity

A large shallow network is worth far less than a small deep one. Focus on engineers you genuinely trust, respect, and are inspired by.

## Part 2: Present to Executives

### 🎯 Core Concept

As your career advances, your impact becomes increasingly constrained by your ability to influence executives. This requires new communication skills — executives consume information differently than your peers.

---

### 🧠 How Executives Think

```
COMMUNICATION MISMATCH:
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

  Executive: Pattern-matcher
  → Asks seemingly random questions until they recognize the situation
  → Give them: Quick answers + let them guide the conversation
  
  Executive: Data-oriented
  → Discounts anything not tied to specific data
  → Give them: Lead with data, have details in appendix
  
  Both types: Preprocessed reality
  → They're used to consuming information in a specific format
  → Wrong format = miscommunication (and often no second chance)
```

### The SCQA Framework

Structure every executive communication's opening paragraph:

| Letter | Element | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **S** | Situation — context | "We've been falling behind competitors for two years. We doubled eng last year but shipped fewer features." |
| **C** | Complication — why it's a problem | "We plan to double engineering again, but expect velocity to decrease further while costs rise significantly." |
| **Q** | Question — the core decision | "Should we proceed with doubling engineering this year?" |
| **A** | Answer — your best recommendation | "We should stop hiring for 6 months and focus on team cohesion." |

A well-structured opening paragraph often sparks the entire discussion. Even if you never present slide 2, the document was worth writing.

### Common Mistakes to Avoid

| Mistake | Why It's Harmful |
|---|---|
| **Fighting feedback** | Executives hold back future feedback; you get less value from the meeting |
| **Evading problems** | Creates credibility loss; executive skepticism once lost is hard to regain |
| **No answer with the question** | Makes them wonder if you need a more senior leader |
| **Academic presentation style** | Not how executives consume information |
| **Fixating on your preferred outcome** | Misses the broader context you don't have; almost no decision is permanent |

### The One Best Tip

> **Send an early draft to an executive attending the meeting and ask them what to change.** If you listen to and apply that feedback, you'll figure out the rest as you go.

## ✅ Key Takeaways

| # | Takeaway |
|---|---|
| 1 | **A personal network of peers** is the most consistent career-growth recommendation from Staff+ engineers |
| 2 | **Quality over quantity** — a few deeply trusted peers beats a large shallow network |
| 3 | **Being visible** (writing, speaking) makes building a network easier — people find you |
| 4 | **Internal networks are underrated** — current coworkers spread across the industry over time |
| 5 | **Executives preprocess information** differently — wrong format = miscommunication |
| 6 | **Use SCQA** to structure executive communications — Situation, Complication, Question, Answer |
| 7 | **Never fight feedback in the room** — collect it, process it after, then follow up |
| 8 | **Send a draft before the meeting** — getting executive feedback early is the single best prep |
