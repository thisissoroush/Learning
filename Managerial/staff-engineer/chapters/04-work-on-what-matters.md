# Chapter 4: Work on What Matters
## *Avoiding the Three Traps That Waste Senior Careers*

---

## 🎯 Core Concept

As your career advances, available work time shrinks (life expands) while expectations for impact grow. The only way to sustain this is ruthless prioritization. This chapter identifies the three most common ways senior engineers waste their time — and the framework for choosing work that actually matters.

> *"I've taken to using the word 'energized' over 'impactful.' 'Impactful' feels company-centric... 'energized' is more inwards-looking."* — Michelle Bu

## 🍿 Trap 1: Snacking

**Snacking** is doing easy, low-impact work. Once you've done the high-impact easy stuff, the remaining options are:
- Hard and high-impact (the right choice)
- Easy and low-impact (snacking)

Snacks give a psychological sense of accomplishment but provide little actual value.

```
IMPACT vs. EFFORT MATRIX:
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

          HIGH IMPACT
               │
               │   Do these first!    │  Hard and important:
               │   Easy wins          │  Staff engineer territory
               │                      │
    ───────────┼──────────────────────┼───────────
               │                      │
               │   SNACKING ← danger  │  Hard and low-impact:
               │   zone for seniors   │  Avoid unless unavoidable
               │                      │
          LOW IMPACT
               
              LOW EFFORT          HIGH EFFORT
```

### The Self-Check

Ask yourself weekly: **What percentage of my hours went to high-impact work?**

In senior roles you have more autonomy to choose your work — which means more room to drift toward comfortable snacks without noticing.

## 💅 Trap 2: Preening

**Preening** is a specific, seductive kind of snacking: **low-impact but high-visibility** work. Many companies conflate high-visibility with high-impact, making preening appear successful when it isn't.

```
PREENING EXAMPLES:
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

  ✗ Polishing a talk for company all-hands instead of fixing the system
  ✗ Writing internal blog posts about work you didn't actually ship
  ✗ Attending high-visibility meetings to be "seen" vs. contributing
  ✗ Sponsoring initiatives that get noticed by leadership but don't deliver

SHORT TERM:    Looks great! Gets recognized in meetings!
LONG TERM:     Work suffers. Real output declines.
               Reputation eventually catches up.
```

### The Preening Trap at the Org Level

Some organizations *reward* preening — where perception management matters more than substance. If you join such an organization:
- Short-term optimization: preen gloriously
- Long-term: your actual skills stagnate, and the organization selects for performers over doers

> Dig into what a company values *before* joining. If leadership consists of people who focus on performative urgency or acts of fealty, expect to be evaluated on those activities.

## 👻 Trap 3: Chasing Ghosts

**Chasing ghosts** is investing heavily in work that *feels* important because it matches your previous experience — but doesn't actually fit the current situation.

```
HOW GHOST-CHASING HAPPENS:
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

  New senior leader joins company
        │
        ▼
  Previous company had Problem X
        │
        ▼
  New company looks superficially similar
        │
        ▼
  "Obviously, we need Solution X!" → massive initiative
        │
        ▼
  Real problem was Y (not X) → initiative fails
  OR: Problem X was real, but solution doesn't fit the culture
```

### The Ego Problem

Ghost-chasing is fueled by ego: "My instincts are right!" But the situation is new. Taking time to understand the status quo before changing it always repays the diligence.

> *"Taking the time to understand the status quo before shifting it will always repay diligence with results."*

## ✅ What to Work On Instead

### 1. Existential Issues (Always First)

If the company is in existential danger — running out of money, facing a stability crisis, navigating a massive external shift — that's always the place to be. Nothing else matters if the company doesn't survive this round.

```
EXISTENTIAL EXAMPLES:
  • Running out of money (runway < 6 months)
  • Critical security breach in production
  • Core product's stability collapsing (Twitter's fail whale era)
  • Regulatory crisis that threatens the license to operate
```

### 2. Where There's Room AND Attention

After existential issues, find work that:
- **Matters to company leadership** (has organizational attention)
- **Has room for your contribution** (isn't already crowded with people)

```
OPTIMAL ZONE:
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

         HIGH COMPANY ATTENTION
               │
               │   OPTIMAL →→→ │  Everyone's here:
               │   Room to do  │  hard to have
               │   great work  │  meaningful impact
    ───────────┼───────────────┼───────────
               │               │
               │   Nobody      │  Low priority
               │   cares:      │  crowded with
               │   unsupported │  indifferent work
               │               │
         LOW COMPANY ATTENTION
               
              ROOM TO WORK         NO ROOM TO WORK
```

### 3. Foster Growth (Chronically Underinvested)

Growing the team around you is almost always underinvested — meaning there's **lots of room** to work there, and the leverage is extraordinary.

> A few hours a week developing the team around you will become your legacy long after your tech specs and pull requests are forgotten.

### 4. Edit (Small Changes, Big Unlocks)

A surprisingly high number of projects are **one small change away** from succeeding. Use your organizational privilege, relationships, and experience to identify and make those edits.

- A quick conversation to reach consensus
- A minor scope change to make a project finishable
- Connecting two teams who didn't know they needed each other

### 5. Finish Things (Chronically Undervalued)

**We only get value from finished projects.** A 90%-done project that never ships delivers zero value. Getting stuck projects across the finish line is high-leverage work.

> *"Getting a project over the finish line is the magical moment it goes from risk to leverage."*

Coaching a teammate on how to rescope their work into something finishable — then lending your privilege to remove friction — can transform a 6-month slog into a 2-week sprint.

### 6. What Only You Can Do

This is the most important category: **work that simply won't happen if you don't do it.**

```
"WHAT ONLY YOU CAN DO" TEST:
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

  Ask yourself:
  "If I don't do this, will it happen?"

  YES → Anyone can do it → delegate or deprioritize
  NO  → This is a candidate for your most important work

  Best case: the intersection of
  ├── What you're exceptionally good at
  └── What you genuinely care about
```

Examples from the book:
- Writing the technology strategy that people will actually follow
- Changing the CEO's mind on how to pay down tech debt
- Convincing a critical candidate to join
- Crafting a discerning API that 10,000 developers will use

## ✅ Key Takeaways

| # | Takeaway |
|---|---|
| 1 | **Snacking feels productive but isn't** — track your high-impact hours deliberately |
| 2 | **Preening is rewarded in some orgs** — but stagnates your actual skills and real output |
| 3 | **Ghost-chasing** is the ego trap for senior leaders — understand first, change second |
| 4 | **Existential issues always come first** — nothing else matters if the company fails |
| 5 | **Find work with room AND attention** — not just one or the other |
| 6 | **Growing people is chronically underinvested** — huge room and huge leverage |
| 7 | **Editing is high-leverage** — small nudges with your privilege can unlock big outcomes |
| 8 | **Finish things** — 90% done is worth 0%; getting to done is magic |
| 9 | **What only you can do** is your most important work — protect this time fiercely |
