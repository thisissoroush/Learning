# Chapter 9: Learn to Never Be Wrong
## *Being Right While Creating Space for Others*

---

## 🎯 Core Concept

Most people know engineers who are never wrong — and who make every discussion a zero-sum debate until their view wins. The goal of this chapter is something different: being right *without* dominating the room. Right in a way that creates space rather than consuming it.

> *"I'm steering and influencing more than saying, 'I've got the authority to just tell you what to do.' I've never seen that style work well."* — Keavy McMinn

---

## 🎯 The Zero-Sum Debate Problem

```
ZERO-SUM DEBATE MINDSET:       COLLABORATIVE DISCUSSION MINDSET:
────────────────────────        ──────────────────────────────────
Goal: Win the argument          Goal: Reach the best outcome
                                
Enters with: "I'm right,        Enters with: "I want to understand
prove me wrong"                  everyone's perspective"

Listens to: Rebuttals           Listens to: Underlying needs & concerns

Result even when "winning":      Result: Room aligns around best answer
Others feel steamrolled          People leave energized
Relationships erode              Relationships strengthen
```

---

## 🔑 Three Core Techniques

### 1. Listening Through Questions

Ask good questions *before* sharing your perspective. The goal: understand the room's views, not interrogate them.

```
BAD QUESTIONS (interrogating):    GOOD QUESTIONS (opening space):
────────────────────────────      ────────────────────────────────
"Why did you choose X?"           "What were the main tradeoffs
(implies X was wrong)             you were weighing?"

"How did you miss Y?"             "What alternatives did you consider?"

"Didn't you consider Z?"          "What would make this decision wrong?"
```

> **Rule:** Ask three good questions before sharing your own perspective. Watch the room shift around you.

### 2. Define the Purpose

Many discussions go nowhere because participants don't agree on what the meeting is for.

When a discussion is unclear, state your understanding as a clarifying question:
> "Just to check — our goal here is to decide whether to postpone the launch by two weeks?"

⚠️ Use this sparingly. Multiple failed reframings almost always end with scheduling another meeting.

### 3. Read the Room

```
ROOM STATE                        RIGHT RESPONSE
──────────────────────────        ──────────────────────────────────
People are close to aligned       Land them on an approach now
People are far apart              Stop pushing; identify a subgroup
                                  to work it offline, or escalate
Someone is blocking               Address privately before the meeting
Everyone's exhausted              Stop and reschedule
```

---

## 🧱 Dealing with Jerks

Sometimes the room has someone who withholds consent, won't compromise, or doesn't listen. Two most effective approaches:

1. **Include someone they can't be a jerk to** — their manager, the CTO
2. **Invest in pre-alignment** — meet with them before the meeting so they feel heard

If it's someone you work with regularly:
- Give them honest feedback, kindly
- Give it a second time
- Document both instances
- If no improvement, communicate concerns to their manager with documentation

> Note: The title shelters you from some of this behavior. If it feels borderline for you, it's likely worse for others around you.

---

## 🌊 Why This Approach Is Actually Faster

```
"BEING RIGHT" APPROACH:       "NEVER BE WRONG" APPROACH:
────────────────────────      ──────────────────────────────────
Feels fast (force agreement)  Feels slow (build understanding)
Builds resentment             Builds partnership
Relationship damage           Relationship strengthened
Often doesn't stick           Decisions hold

Long-term: SLOWER             Long-term: FASTER
```

More complex projects are derailed by personal conflict than by technical complexity. Replacing tension with partnership gets things done faster over time.

---

## ✅ Key Takeaways

| # | Takeaway |
|---|---|
| 1 | **Enter every meeting to understand**, not to win |
| 2 | **Ask 3 good questions** before sharing your view — this alone shifts room dynamics |
| 3 | **Define the purpose** when discussions are unclear — saves hours of circular debate |
| 4 | **Read the room** — don't force agreement when people are too far apart |
| 5 | **Deal with jerks** by pre-alignment or including authority, not by out-arguing them |
| 6 | **The collaborative approach is faster** — less relationship damage means faster subsequent decisions |
| 7 | **Longevity as a senior leader** depends on maintaining relationships, not just standout successes |
