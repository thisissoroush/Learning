# Chapter 12: Getting the Title (Where You Are & By Switching)
## *The Practical Roadmap to the Staff+ Promotion*

---

## 🎯 Core Concept

About two-thirds of Staff engineers get the title via internal promotion; one-third by changing companies. Both are valid. The key is being deliberate: understand where you are in the process, what's blocking you, and which path gives you the best chance.

> *"Often reaching Staff is a combination of luck, timing, and work."* — Bert Fan

---

## 📋 The Promotion Packet

Start your promotion packet **early** — long before you think you'll be promoted. Use it as a map, not a capstone.

**Packet structure:**
```
1. Your Staff-level projects
   └── What, why, what was complex, link to design docs

2. High-leverage org improvements
   └── What you changed beyond your direct team

3. Quantifiable impact
   └── $10M revenue? 20% fewer support tickets? Be specific.

4. Mentorship record
   └── Who, what they accomplished

5. Glue work contribution
   └── What invisible work do you do? What's the impact?

6. Advocates
   └── Who knows your work? What do they value about it?

7. Gaps to address
   └── Known weaknesses + your plan for each
```

**Iteration process:** Draft → Edit after 2 days → Share with trusted peers → Share with manager → Review every quarter

## 🤝 Find and Activate Your Sponsor

Your **direct manager is your most critical sponsor**. Without their active advocacy in promotion meetings, you won't get promoted into a leadership role.

```
SPONSOR ACTIVATION STEPS:
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

  1. Explicitly share your goal: "I'm working toward Staff"
  
  2. Ask specific questions:
     "If I don't get promoted this cycle, what are the most 
      likely causes?"
     (Not: "What should I do to become Staff?")
  
  3. Make their sponsorship easy — align pieces for them;
     don't assume they'll do all the heavy lifting
  
  4. Build relationship with skip-level too — they're often
     in the room where promotion decisions are made
  
  5. Stay aligned with their initiatives — show up when needed,
     not just before promotion season
```

## 🏗️ The Staff Project

Most engineers don't need a Staff project. But if your company does (often informally required), understand what makes one:

| Characteristic | Description |
|---|---|
| **Complex & ambiguous** | Starts with a poorly scoped but important problem |
| **Divided stakeholders** | Multiple parties with conflicting views on approach |
| **Named bet** | Senior leadership talks about it at all-hands; failure is visible |

Even if not required, Staff projects are valuable because they stretch you in ways other work doesn't.

---

## 🚪 Get in the Room (And Stay There)

**To get in:** Bring something useful the room doesn't already have + have a sponsor who wants you there.

**Stay by:** Being aligned, concise, low-friction, coming prepared, pulling your weight on low-status tasks.

**Get kicked out by:** Being dogmatic, withholding consent, dominating the conversation, embarrassing your sponsor, or not showing up consistently.

---

## 👁️ Be Visible

You can't get promoted into a leadership role if leadership doesn't know your work. Ways to create visibility:
- Write and distribute architecture docs and tech specs
- Lead forums: architecture reviews, all-hands, learning circles
- Share weekly notes of your work to your team
- Be a genuine cheerleader for peers' work
- Attend office hours; build executive relationships

> **The goal:** Be known for good things while minimizing organizational bandwidth consumed doing so.

---

## 🔄 When to Switch Companies

Consider switching when:
- You've been "almost promoted" multiple times with a moving criteria line
- Your internal reputation is damaged
- There's no budget/headcount for another Staff engineer
- Your team is so senior that your impact isn't distinguishable from your peers

**When switching:** Find companies where you can do a "bias arbitrage" — where your specific expertise, experience, or background is uniquely valued. Interview until you find one.

**Before switching:** Try transferring internally; it preserves your network while getting many switching benefits.

### Finding the Right Company

| Factor | What to Look For |
|---|---|
| **Archetype fit** | Does the company support your preferred archetype? |
| **Meritocrat vs Proceduralist** | Know which you thrive in |
| **Growth rate** | Fast-growth = new Staff slots opening; slow-growth = wait for vacancy |
| **Sponsor available** | Is there someone who believes in you and will fight for you? |
| **Durability** | Will the company exist in 5 years? Will your sponsor still be there? |
| **Pace** | Matches what you can sustainably give? |

## ✅ Key Takeaways

| # | Takeaway |
|---|---|
| 1 | **Start your promotion packet early** — use it as a map, not a capstone |
| 2 | **Your manager is your most critical sponsor** — without their advocacy, no promotion |
| 3 | **Ask specific questions** about your gaps — not "what should I do" but "what would block me?" |
| 4 | **Staff projects aren't required**, but they're valuable for self-development |
| 5 | **Get in the room by bringing unique value** — and stay by being aligned and low-friction |
| 6 | **Visibility is necessary** — leadership must know your work to promote you into leadership |
| 7 | **Switching companies is legitimate** — often the fastest path to the Staff title |
| 8 | **Find companies where your specific expertise is disproportionately valued** |
