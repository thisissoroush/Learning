# Chapter 10: Create Space for Others
## *From Essential to Adjacent — Growing the Organization Beyond Yourself*

---

## 🎯 Core Concept

One of the best measures of long-term Staff+ success: the organization increasingly benefits from — but doesn't *rely upon* — your contributions. The transition from "the person who does it" to "the person who makes it possible for others to do it" is one of the most important shifts in a senior engineering career.

> *"At this point, I spend less time advocating for specific technologies and more time empowering others to advocate for the technologies and programs that they think are important."* — Michelle Bu

---

## 🔄 The Transition: Essential → Adjacent

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                      THE ESSENTIAL → ADJACENT SHIFT                      │
│                                                                          │
│  ESSENTIAL (before):               ADJACENT (after):                    │
│  ────────────────────              ────────────────────                  │
│  Organization relies on you        Organization benefits from you       │
│  You're the bottleneck             Others are empowered                 │
│  Decisions wait for you            Decisions happen without you         │
│  Your absence = blocked work       Your absence = normal operation      │
│                                                                          │
│  Risk: Company constrained         Benefit: Company scales              │
│  by your limits                    beyond your limits                   │
│                                                                          │
│  The hard part: This requires      The reward: You can now work on      │
│  giving up control and credit      what only you can do                 │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

## 💬 Create Space in Discussions

Expand your definition of "a good meeting" from "ended quickly with my answer" to "others contributed and grew."

**Practical techniques:**
- **Shift toward asking questions** — helps others contribute and avoids missteps
- **Pull in silent voices** — invite exactly one specific person at a time (not "anyone else?")
- **Be the one who takes notes** — destigmatizes note-taking and frees others to talk
- **Identify missing voices** — if someone should be here, make sure they're invited next time

When you make a key contribution, ask: *"What would need to happen for someone else to make this contribution next time?"*

## 🎯 Create Space in Decisions

```
TECHNIQUES FOR TRANSFERRING DECISION-MAKING:
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

  1. Write it down — document your reasoning so others can learn the process,
     not just receive the answer

  2. Circulate early — share thinking before you've crystallized a position;
     easier to incorporate feedback when you're still open

  3. Separate style from substance — don't give feedback that won't
     meaningfully improve success; let go of stylistic preferences

  4. Don't show value — weigh in because it matters, not to prove seniority

  5. Change your mind — publicly, when colleagues make good points;
     one of the greatest signs of respect for your coworkers
```

## 🤝 Sponsorship: Making the Work Theirs

The ultimate form of creating space: **sponsorship**. When important work comes to you, ask: "Who could be both successful with and grown by this work?"

```
SPONSORSHIP PROCESS:
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

  Work identified as critical
        │
        ▼
  Ask: "Who could grow from this?"
        │
        ▼
  Help them scope and scaffold the project
  ├── What would your approach be?
  ├── What concerns should they think through?
  └── Who are the key stakeholders to involve early?
        │
        ▼
  Let it be THEIRS — counsel and support,
  but let them take their approach
        │
        ▼
  Outcome A: Goes poorly → They learn
  Outcome B: Goes well → You learn something new
```

**Important:** Keep a sponsorship journal. Ensure you're sponsoring others at least a few times per month.

---

## ⚠️ What Happens If You Don't

Companies where one person is essential but not adjacent:
- Thrive temporarily while that person is there
- Are constrained by that person's limitations
- Can't grow beyond what one person can hold

> *"The only way to remain a long-term leader of a genuinely successful company is to continually create space for others to take the recognition, reward, and work that got you to where you're currently sitting."*

---

## ✅ Key Takeaways

| # | Takeaway |
|---|---|
| 1 | **Measure your success** by how well the organization works without your direct involvement |
| 2 | **Good meetings don't need you** — the best meetings are ones you didn't need to attend |
| 3 | **Pull in specific people**, not "everyone" — targeted inclusion beats open invitations |
| 4 | **Document your reasoning**, not just your answers — others learn the process, not just the outcome |
| 5 | **Sponsorship is making work theirs** — not just involving them, but handing over ownership |
| 6 | **Let the work be theirs** — that means tolerating approaches different from yours |
| 7 | **The essential-person trap kills company growth** — your legacy should outlast your tenure |
