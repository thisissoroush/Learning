# Chapter 4: Managing People
## *Entry-level management — the most humbling career restart you'll ever make*

---

## 🎯 Core Concept

Becoming a manager is **not a promotion** — it's a career change. The skills that made you a great engineer (deep focus, technical precision, individual output) are now secondary. Your new job is to multiply the output of your entire team by developing each person on it.

> "New engineering managers think of the job as a promotion, giving them seniority on engineering tasks. This is a great approach for ensuring they remain junior managers."
> — Marc Hedlund

---

## 🚀 Starting a New Reporting Relationship

The first weeks with a new report set the tone for everything that follows. Don't waste them on status updates — use them to build trust.

### Questions to Ask Every New Report

```
GET TO KNOW HOW TO MANAGE THEM:

  "How do you like to receive praise — public or private?"
  "What's your preferred format for critical feedback?"
  "What manager behaviors have bothered you in the past?"
  "How will I know when you're in a bad mood?"
  "Do you have career goals I should know about?"
  "Any surprises since joining — good or bad?"
```

### The 30/60/90-Day Plan

Set clear, measurable expectations from day one:

```
30 DAYS:  Get set up, understand the codebase, ship one small fix
60 DAYS:  Complete a feature, participate in team ceremonies
90 DAYS:  Be independently productive, know who to ask for what
```

A 90-day plan serves double duty: it helps new hires succeed *and* it helps you catch mis-hires early before the relationship calcifies.

## 📅 The 1-1: Your Most Important Management Tool

> "Regular 1-1s are like oil changes; if you skip them, plan to get stranded on the side of the highway at the worst possible time."
> — Marc Hedlund

### Scheduling Rules

- **Default: Weekly.** Adjust only by mutual agreement.
- Avoid Mondays and Fridays (long-weekend risk).
- Protect their maker schedule — don't put 1-1s mid-deep-work-block.
- If you must reduce frequency, **never eliminate entirely**.

### Four 1-1 Styles

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  STYLE            │ WHEN TO USE          │ WATCH OUT FOR   │
├───────────────────┼──────────────────────┼─────────────────┤
│ 📋 To-Do List     │ Task-oriented teams  │ Can feel cold   │
│                   │ Clear agendas needed │ or mechanical   │
├───────────────────┼──────────────────────┼─────────────────┤
│ ☕ Catch-up       │ Default / general    │ Can drift into  │
│                   │ Fluid discussion     │ therapy session │
├───────────────────┼──────────────────────┼─────────────────┤
│ 🎯 Feedback       │ Performance issues   │ Don't wait for  │
│                   │ Career development   │ review season   │
├───────────────────┼──────────────────────┼─────────────────┤
│ 📊 Progress       │ Side projects        │ Boring for most │
│   Report          │ Senior reports up    │ ICs — avoid     │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Key rule:** 1-1s should NOT be status updates. If it's only status, do that via email and use the time for real conversation.

## 📣 Continuous Feedback: The Engine of Growth

Feedback should flow constantly, not just at review time. Saving it up is harmful — by the time you deliver it, the moment has passed and behavior has solidified.

### The Feedback Loop

```
      OBSERVE                    RECOGNIZE
         ↓                          ↓
  Watch for achievements    Give specific praise
  and problem patterns      publicly (if preferred)
         ↓                          ↓
      COACH                    CORRECT
         ↓                          ↓
  Ask: "What would you      Address issues quickly
  do differently?"          and privately
```

### Three Steps to Continuous Feedback

1. **Know your people** — their goals, strengths, where they're headed
2. **Observe your people** — pay attention in meetings, PRs, interactions
3. **Provide lightweight, regular feedback** — weekly at minimum; don't save it all up

## 🎖️ Micromanager vs. Effective Delegator

One of the most common failure modes for new managers is micromanagement — often triggered by anxiety, not malice.

```
MICROMANAGER                    EFFECTIVE DELEGATOR
                                
  ❌ Attends every standup       ✅ Attends selectively
  ❌ Reassigns tickets           ✅ Coaches on prioritization
  ❌ Overrides technical calls   ✅ Guides, then trusts
  ❌ Demands hourly updates      ✅ Sets milestones, checks in
  ❌ "Do it my way"              ✅ "Here's the goal — you decide how"
  ❌ Snatches back ownership     ✅ Stays available without hovering
```

**The story of Jane vs. Sharell:**
- **Jane** delegated a project, panicked, took it back from Sanjay. Project shipped — but Sanjay became disengaged and stopped trying. A star became a low performer.
- **Sharell** gave Beth clear responsibilities, stayed available, helped cut scope at the end. Beth left the project more confident and ready for bigger challenges.

> Delegation ≠ Abdication. You're still responsible. You're just not doing it yourself.

## 📝 Performance Reviews: Done Right

Reviews go wrong in predictable ways:
- Written at the last minute without reflection
- Focused only on recent events (recency bias)
- Vague generalizations without concrete examples
- Filled with bias disguised as objective feedback

### The Right Way to Write a Review

```
✅ Start early — block uninterrupted time
✅ Review the full year, not just the last month
✅ Use concrete examples and quotes from peer feedback
✅ Celebrate strengths FULLY before discussing improvements
✅ Focus on 2-3 clear improvement themes, not a laundry list
✅ No surprises — if it's in the review, they've heard it before
✅ Schedule 60+ minutes for the conversation
```

### When Delivering the Review

Give it to them the night before so they can process it emotionally. Then walk through it together, spending real time on the strengths section — don't let them rush past it to get to the "criticism."

## 🌱 Cultivating Careers: Your Promotion Playbook

As a manager, you are a critical path for your reports' career growth. You need to:

1. **Know the promotion process** at your company cold
2. **Identify promotion-worthy projects** and assign them strategically
3. **Give stretch assignments** to people approaching the next level
4. **Make the case** — gather evidence, write the packet, advocate

```
PROMOTION EVIDENCE CHECKLIST:
  
  □ Projects shipped independently
  □ Leadership demonstrated beyond their role
  □ Positive peer feedback
  □ Metrics and business impact
  □ Participation in hiring, onboarding, etc.
  □ Feedback incorporated over time
```

## ⚠️ Firing for Underperformance

This is one of the hardest and most important management skills. The key rules:

```
RULE 1: No surprises
  If they're surprised by the firing, you failed earlier.
  Feedback must be ongoing, documented, and clear.

RULE 2: Start early
  Don't wait until you're furious. Address issues at first signs.

RULE 3: Write it down
  Give feedback in writing. Have them acknowledge receipt.
  This protects both of you and makes expectations crystal clear.

RULE 4: Don't put anyone on a PIP you'd be happy to lose
  Most smart engineers read a PIP as "time to find a new job"
  and leave within weeks, even if they meet the goals.
```

## 💡 Key Takeaways

| # | Takeaway |
|---|---|
| 1 | **"New manager" is an entry-level job** — bring humility, not authority |
| 2 | **1-1s are non-negotiable** — skip them and you'll be blindsided |
| 3 | **Continuous feedback > annual reviews** — don't save up the hard stuff |
| 4 | **Delegation ≠ abandonment** — stay involved at the right level |
| 5 | **Performance reviews should have zero surprises** — if it's new to them, you're late |
| 6 | **Firing is part of the job** — done compassionately and early, it's kindness |
| 7 | **You are your team's career advocate** — learn the promotion game and play it for them |
