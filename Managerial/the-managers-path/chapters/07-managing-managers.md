# Chapter 7: Managing Managers

> *"The people who are managing managers are no longer in the room where it happens — they need to learn how to be effective from a distance."*

---

## 🎯 Core Concept

Managing managers is a **quantum leap** in complexity. You've gone from seeing problems directly to receiving them filtered through another layer. Your job is no longer about managing work — it's about **managing people who manage work**. The distance creates blind spots, and your biggest challenge is knowing what you don't know.

---

## 📊 The Visibility Problem

```
OLD WORLD (Managing Teams):
You ──→ Engineers ──→ Code/Output
        ↑ You can see everything directly

NEW WORLD (Managing Managers):
You ──→ Manager A ──→ Team A ──→ Output
     ↘  Manager B ──→ Team B ──→ Output
        ↑ You only see filtered info
```

**The danger:** Problems on teams can be hidden — intentionally or not — by the managers between you and the work.

---

## 🔭 Skip-Level Meetings: Your X-Ray Vision

### What Is a Skip-Level?
A meeting between you and the **people who report to your direct reports** — skipping one level of hierarchy.

```
       You
        │
    ┌───┴───┐
 Manager   Manager
    │         │
  Team A    Team B
    ↑
  Skip-level = You meeting directly with Team A
```

### Why They Matter
- Detect problems before they explode
- Hear the ground-level reality, not the polished version
- Build relationships that survive manager turnover
- Catch managers who are "managing up" at the expense of their teams

### Skip-Level Meeting Formats

| Format | Frequency | Best For |
|--------|-----------|----------|
| 1-on-1 | Quarterly | Sensitive topics, individual feedback |
| Group lunch | Monthly | Team dynamics, morale pulse |
| Office hours | Ongoing | Low barrier, self-selected topics |

### 💬 Sample Questions for Skip-Level 1-1s

```
🔍 Work & Project Health:
  • "What are you most excited about right now?"
  • "What's slowing your team down most?"
  • "Are there opportunities you think we're missing?"

👥 Manager Effectiveness:
  • "Is there anything your manager could do differently?"
  • "Do you feel you understand your team's goals?"
  • "What's keeping you from doing your best work?"

🌡️ Culture & Morale:
  • "How happy are you working here, on a scale of 1-10?"
  • "What would make this a more fun place to work?"
```

> ⚠️ **Anti-pattern:** The "open door policy" trap — telling people "come to me anytime" doesn't work. People won't walk in. You must go to them.

## ✅ Manager Accountability

### The Core Principle
Your managers exist to make your job easier. If they're hiding problems and telling you what you want to hear, they're failing — and you're failing too.

### Three Hard Scenarios (And Who's Responsible)

#### Scenario 1: Unstable Product Roadmap
```
Symptom: Team is unproductive, product keeps changing goals

Root cause: Manager hasn't pushed back on product chaos

✅ Manager's job: Identify that constant changes are causing 
   team harm → negotiate with product → escalate to you if needed

❌ Not acceptable: "Product keeps changing things, nothing we can do"
```

#### Scenario 2: Tech Lead Down a Rabbit Hole
```
Symptom: Core design doc unfinished for weeks, work piling up

Root cause: Manager not intervening in tech lead's perfectionism

✅ Manager's job: Surface the bottleneck, bring in external 
   senior help, set timeline expectations

❌ Not acceptable: "The tech lead says it's complex, I trust them"
```

#### Scenario 3: Full-Time Firefighting
```
Symptom: Team drowning in legacy system fires, no roadmap progress

Root cause: Manager hasn't triaged support burden or escalated

✅ Manager's job: Create a plan, reduce support, request headcount,
   show you a path forward

❌ Not acceptable: Silently watching the team burn out
```

### What "Accountability" Actually Means
Accountability doesn't mean blame. It means:
1. **Managers own the diagnosis** of what's wrong
2. **They bring you clear problems**, not vague complaints
3. **They propose paths forward**, even when they need your help
4. **You support them** by removing blockers above their pay grade

## 🚨 Common Manager Anti-Patterns

### 🎭 The People Pleaser

```
Signs you're managing a people pleaser:

✓ Team loves them as a person
✓ Team is increasingly frustrated by their decisions
✓ They never push back on new requests
✓ They overpromise and underdeliver (repeatedly)
✓ They hide problems from you
✓ Outstanding promises stack up with no follow-through
```

**The two flavors:**

| Type | Behavior | Problem |
|------|----------|---------|
| Team Pleaser (Marcus) | Spends all time listening, validating | Amplifies drama, makes promises he can't keep |
| External Pleaser | Manages up brilliantly, shields team | Team left uninformed, burnout from overcommitment |

**How to help a people pleaser:**
- Help them see the pattern — awareness is step one
- Create external structures (promotion criteria, roadmap process) so they can point outside themselves
- Give them partners who handle difficult decisions
- Affirm that the team needs a manager who delivers, not just one who listens

---

### 🕹️ The Control Freak (New Manager Variant)

A first-time manager who mistakes authority for management:

```
❌ Control Freak Behaviors:
  • Assigns all work, makes all decisions
  • Skips your 1-1s, hides what team is doing  
  • Fights peers for headcount and ownership
  • Team has no autonomy whatsoever

👀 Warning signs:
  • Senior engineers leaving the team
  • Manager resistant to sharing status
  • Peers complaining about "turf protection"
```

## 🧑‍🏫 Managing First-Time Managers

New managers are exciting and terrifying. They want to succeed but often don't know what success looks like.

### Your Coaching Checklist for New Managers

```
Week 1-4:
□ Are they holding regular 1-1s?
□ Do they know what to talk about in 1-1s?
□ Have they introduced themselves to the team properly?

Month 1-3:
□ Are they giving feedback (positive and corrective)?
□ Are they delegating their old IC work?
□ Are they learning about the team's goals and blockers?

Month 3-6:
□ Are they accountable for team delivery?
□ Are they coming to you with problems (not hiding them)?
□ Are they building trust with their team?
```

### The Overwork Signal
```
New manager working 60+ hours/week?

Possible causes:
  A) Hasn't handed off old IC responsibilities ← Most common
  B) Taking on too many meetings 
  C) Playing control freak — doing team's work

Your move: Help them identify what to let go of
```

### Getting Them External Training
- Company HR programs for new managers
- Engineering leadership conferences
- Management books and peer communities
- Pair them with experienced managers as mentors

## 🧭 Managing Experienced Managers

Experienced managers come with skills — and baggage.

### The Culture Fit Problem
```
Example: Hiring a seasoned manager from BigCorp into your startup

BigCorp Manager might:
  • Expect waterfall-style planning
  • Need sign-off from 5 people for decisions
  • Treat junior staff as "resources"
  • Ship software quarterly, not daily

Your startup needs:
  • Fast iteration
  • Collaborative decision-making
  • Servant leadership
  • Daily shipping mindset

→ Culture mismatch = team attrition or manager failure
```

**Rule:** Technical expertise in a domain doesn't compensate for cultural incompatibility. Screen for values, not just skills.

### Coaching Experienced Managers
- Don't lecture — collaborate
- Challenge them to grow strategically, not just operationally
- Use them as advisors on organizational decisions
- Help them build cross-company networks

## 🏢 Hiring Managers: The Interview Process

### What to Evaluate

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│           Manager Interview Areas             │
├──────────────┬───────────────────────────────┤
│ 1-1 Skills   │ Role-play a 1-1 with real      │
│              │ problems from your team        │
├──────────────┼───────────────────────────────┤
│ Coaching     │ Describe a struggling employee │
│              │ you coached — what happened?   │
├──────────────┼───────────────────────────────┤
│ Philosophy   │ "What's your management        │
│              │ philosophy?" (No answer = 🚩)  │
├──────────────┼───────────────────────────────┤
│ Technical    │ Architecture/design questions  │
│              │ to ensure team credibility     │
├──────────────┼───────────────────────────────┤
│ Culture Fit  │ Values alignment, working      │
│              │ style, hierarchy expectations  │
└──────────────┴───────────────────────────────┘
```

### Always Do Reference Checks
Ask references:
- "What does this person do really well?"
- "What drives you crazy about working with them?"
- "Would you work for or with them again?"
- "Tell me about a time they failed — how did they handle it?"

> 💡 References chosen by the candidate still reveal a lot if you ask the right questions.

## 🔬 Debugging Dysfunctional Organizations

When a team under one of your managers isn't performing, treat it like a software bug:

```
Step 1: Form a hypothesis
  "I think the team is slow because the tech lead is blocked"

Step 2: Check the data
  • Look at git commits, ticket velocity, incident logs
  • Read team chat — is it energized or silent?
  • Check calendars — are they in meetings 40 hours/week?

Step 3: Observe the team
  • Sit in their standup/planning meetings
  • Are people engaged or checked out?
  • Who's doing all the talking?

Step 4: Ask questions
  • "What are your team's goals?" (Can they answer this?)
  • "What's blocking you most right now?"

Step 5: Check team dynamics
  • Do people collaborate or work in silos?
  • Any banter, or purely transactional?

Step 6: Jump in to help
  • Work with the manager — don't just point at the problem
```

> ⚠️ **Schrödinger's Team:** Your presence changes team behavior. Account for this when observing.

## 📅 Setting Expectations and Delivering on Schedule

### The Estimation Problem
Engineers resist estimating beyond a 2-week sprint. But the business needs a rough view of the future.

**Your strategy:**
```
For short work (< 2 weeks):
  → Agile sprint planning is sufficient

For medium work (2-8 weeks):
  → Double your estimate, flag uncertainty clearly

For long projects (8+ weeks):
  → Invest in real planning time before committing
  → Update estimates proactively when things slip
  → Never let leadership be surprised
```

### When Projects Run Late

```
❌ Wrong response: "Why is this taking so long?!" (blame)

✅ Right response:
  1. Surface the delay proactively before it's asked about
  2. Show the plan you have to recover
  3. Identify scope to cut (features, not quality corners)
  4. Be honest about what's realistic
```

## 🗺️ Handling Roadmap Uncertainty

Strategy changes. Projects get cancelled. Teams get reorganized.

**Survival strategies:**
1. **Be realistic about change cycles** — if your company pivots every summer, plan for it
2. **Break big projects into smaller milestones** — ship value even if the grand vision changes
3. **Don't overpromise "later"** — the future roadmap is always crowded
4. **Reserve 20% for sustaining work** — technical debt, refactoring, fixes
5. **Articulate the value of technical projects** — treat them like product initiatives with clear ROI

## 🧲 Staying Technically Relevant as Senior Manager

You're not coding much anymore. But you still need technical credibility.

### Ways to Stay Sharp

| Activity | Benefit |
|----------|---------|
| Read code reviews | See what's being built and how |
| Ask engineers to explain unknown areas | Build understanding + shows humility |
| Attend postmortems | Reveal systemic problems you'd otherwise miss |
| Follow industry trends | Know when to push for new practices |
| Build peer network externally | Real talk vs. blog hype |

### Your Technical Responsibilities Now

```
1. Oversee technical investment
   → Are we betting on the right technologies?

2. Ask informed questions
   → "Why does this team need more engineers?"
   → "What's blocking progress on this system?"

3. Explain tradeoffs
   → Between technical debt and new features
   → Between speed and stability

4. Make specific requests
   → Filter out bad ideas before they reach engineers
   → Direct focus toward the highest-leverage work
```
