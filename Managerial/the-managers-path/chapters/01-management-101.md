# Chapter 1: Management 101
## *Learning to be managed — and what good management actually looks like*

---

## 🎯 Core Concept

Before you can manage others, you need to understand what it means **to be managed well**. Most engineers have never experienced truly good management, so their mental model of what a manager should do is either "leave me alone" or "stay out of my way." This chapter raises the bar.

> Your experience of being managed is the foundation on which you'll build your own management philosophy.

## 🔑 What a Good Manager Actually Does

### 1. 🗓️ Regular 1-on-1 Meetings (1-1s)

**Two purposes:**
- **Human connection** — knowing you as a person, not just a resource
- **Private communication channel** — a predictable space for anything you need to discuss

| Bad 1-1 | Good 1-1 |
|---|---|
| Status update monologue | Two-way dialogue |
| Frequently cancelled | Reliably scheduled |
| Manager controls the agenda | You both bring topics |
| Feels like a waste of time | Feels like a resource |

> 💡 **Pro tip:** If your 1-1s are pure status updates, try moving that info to email/chat and use the time for actual conversation.

---

### 2. 💬 Feedback — Timely and Honest

Feedback isn't just performance reviews. It's the ongoing signal that tells you whether you're on track.

```
FEEDBACK TIMING MODEL:
                                                    
  Praise  →  PUBLIC  (reinforces good behavior for team)
                                                    
  Critique →  PRIVATE  (delivered quickly, not saved for reviews)
                                                    
  Bad habit →  ADDRESS EARLY  (easier to fix when fresh)
```

**The key insight:** The only thing worse than getting corrective feedback is *not* getting it — and finding out 6 months later during a review.

---

### 3. 🎓 Training & Career Growth

Your manager is your liaison to:
- Training budgets and conference attendance
- Understanding the promotion process
- Getting visibility on the right projects

> ⚠️ **Important:** Your manager cannot read your mind. If you want a promotion, **ask explicitly** and ask what specific things you need to demonstrate.

## 🧠 How to Be Managed Well (Your Responsibility)

Being managed isn't passive. You have real agency here.

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│           YOUR ROLE AS A DIRECT REPORT              │
│                                                     │
│  1. Know what you want  →  Think about your goals   │
│  2. Own your career     →  Ask for what you need    │
│  3. Give your manager a break  →  They're human too │
│  4. Choose wisely       →  Manager matters when     │
│                             evaluating a job        │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### Spend Time Thinking About What You Want

No one can tell you what will make you happy. This is entirely your responsibility to figure out. Your manager can show you opportunities — but you have to know what you're looking for.

**Career uncertainty is normal.** Most people feel it in these windows:
- First 2–5 years out of school
- After climbing the technical ladder
- After climbing the management ladder
- Every 5–10 years, likely forever

### You Are Responsible for Yourself

| If you want this... | Do this |
|---|---|
| To work on a specific project | Ask for it |
| A raise | Ask for it explicitly |
| A promotion | Ask what you need to achieve it |
| Feedback | Seek it out |
| Work-life balance | Set your own boundaries |
| Solutions to problems | Bring proposed solutions, not just problems |

## 🎭 The Good Manager / Bad Manager Spectrum

Here are the manager archetypes you'll encounter in the wild:

| Type | Behavior | Impact |
|---|---|---|
| 🌟 **Great Manager** | Invests in your growth, gives feedback, clears your path | You thrive |
| 😐 **Benign Neglect** | Leaves you alone, no feedback | You survive but don't grow |
| 🔴 **Micromanager** | Questions every decision, no autonomy | You feel disrespected |
| ⚫ **Neglectful** | Ignores you until review season | You get blindsided |
| 💣 **Abusive** | Uses you as a target | You leave |

---

## 💼 Real Example: Choosing a Manager

> Imagine two job offers:
>
> - **Company A:** Exciting product, great pay, unknown manager
> - **Company B:** Decent product, slightly less pay, manager who has promoted 3 people in 2 years, has a reputation for investing in engineers
>
> **The book's advice:** Strongly weigh Company B. A strong manager can make or break your career trajectory. They get you promoted, get you visibility, and can open doors even after you stop working for them.

---

## 🔍 Assess Your Own Experience

Ask yourself these questions honestly:

- [ ] Do you have regular 1-1s with your manager? Do you come prepared with topics?
- [ ] Does your manager know something personal about you — your life outside work?
- [ ] Have you received specific, actionable feedback in the last month?
- [ ] Do you know what you'd need to do to get promoted?
- [ ] Have you told your manager what you want to work on?
