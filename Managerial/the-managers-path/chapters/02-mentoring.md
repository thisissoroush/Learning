# Chapter 2: Mentoring
## *Your first taste of management — guiding someone else without derailing yourself*

---

## 🎯 Core Concept

Mentoring is usually the **first unofficial act of people management** for engineers. It's low-stakes enough to learn from mistakes, but high-impact enough to shape someone's career trajectory and your company's hiring pipeline. Done well, it's one of the highest-leverage things a senior engineer can do.

> The mentor gets to experience responsibility for another person. The mentee gets focused attention without competing for it.

## 🎓 Mentoring an Intern

Intern mentoring is a **pipeline investment**. The intern will go back to their university and tell 20 people what it was like to work at your company. That word-of-mouth shapes your next recruiting class.

### The Intern Mentoring Checklist

```
BEFORE THEY ARRIVE:
  ✅ Know their start date
  ✅ Desk, computer, and system access ready
  ✅ A project defined (not just "help out")

FIRST WEEK:
  ✅ Sit with them as much as possible
  ✅ Help them install tools, check out code
  ✅ Touch base multiple times per day

DURING THE INTERNSHIP:
  ✅ Break the project into milestones
  ✅ Calibrate check-in frequency to their needs
  ✅ Give them room to try, adjust when stuck

AT THE END:
  ✅ Have them present their work to the team
  ✅ Write a clear hire/no-hire recommendation
```

### Project Selection Rule of Thumb

> For a 10-week internship, give them a project you think would take a new hire **about 5 weeks**. This leaves room for onboarding activities and gives them room to surprise you by finishing early.

### The 3 Core Mentoring Skills (Practice Ground)

#### 1. 👂 Listen Carefully

Listening is the first and most basic skill of managing people. When your mentee speaks, ask yourself:
- Am I thinking about what I want to say next instead of hearing them?
- Am I picking up on their body language and tone?
- Do they seem confused but too afraid to say so?

```
LISTENING MISTAKE           VS.      ACTIVE LISTENING
                                     
"I know what they mean,              "Let me repeat that back
I'll just answer..."                  to make sure I understand"

"That's a dumb question"             "That's a good question —
                                      let me draw it on the board"

Waiting for them to finish           Noticing when they seem lost
so you can talk                      and asking questions
```

#### 2. 📢 Communicate Clearly

If you expect someone to research before asking you, **say that explicitly**. Don't let your expectations live only in your head.

**Example:** Instead of being frustrated when your intern asks basic questions without trying first, say:
> *"Before you ask me something, I'd like you to spend 30 minutes trying to find the answer yourself. Then come to me with what you found and where you got stuck."*

#### 3. 🎛️ Calibrate Your Response

Different interns need different levels of support:
- Some need daily check-ins
- Some need weekly guidance
- Some will blow past your expectations and need more challenge
- Some will struggle and need more structure

Spend the first 2 weeks learning which type you have, then adapt.

## 👋 Mentoring a New Hire

New hire mentoring has a longer time horizon and a different goal: **helping someone integrate into the company's culture, networks, and work patterns**.

### What Good New Hire Mentoring Looks Like

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│              THE NEW HIRE MENTOR'S JOB               │
│                                                      │
│  TECHNICAL          CULTURAL            NETWORK      │
│                                                      │
│  • Set up dev env   • Explain unspoken  • Introduce  │
│  • Walk code base     rules               to key     │
│  • Pair program     • Help navigate       people     │
│  • Review their       team norms        • Include    │
│    first PRs        • Translate           in your    │
│  • Update docs        company jargon     networks    │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### The Onboarding Documentation Power Move

Have every new hire:
1. Work through the onboarding docs
2. Update those docs wherever they're wrong, outdated, or confusing
3. Share what they found

This sends a powerful message: *"You have the power and obligation to improve things here."*

## ⚠️ The Alpha Geek: A Mentoring Anti-Pattern

The **alpha geek** is one of the most common dysfunctions to emerge when engineers become mentors or managers.

```
ALPHA GEEK TRAITS:
                                                        
  🧠 Needs to be the smartest person in the room
  ⚔️  Views dissent as a personal threat
  🏆 Takes credit for team wins, none for losses
  😤 Belittles people who make mistakes
  🔒 Hoards information to maintain an edge
  🙅 Can't let others get glory without sharing it
```

**At their best:** Highly knowledgeable, technically impressive, can be inspiring
**At their worst:** Creates a culture of fear, drives away talent, undermines teammates

> If you find yourself eager to point out what someone did wrong, reluctant to admit when someone else has a good idea, or always needing the last word — you may be showing alpha geek tendencies.

**Mentoring is the antidote.** When you adopt the goal of *helping someone succeed in the way that works best for them*, you're forced to let go of your own ego. That shift is the beginning of real leadership.

## 💡 Tips for Managers Setting Up Mentoring Programs

If you're assigning mentors to new hires or interns, avoid these common mistakes:

| Mistake | Why It Fails | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Treating it as low-status "soft" work | The mentor deprioritizes it | Recognize it as real work with real impact |
| Assuming only "like mentors like" | Limits diversity, limits quality | Match based on *skills needed*, not demographics |
| No clear goals for the relationship | Both parties feel lost | Define what success looks like up front |
| Assigning it to someone overloaded | Mentor checks out | Choose someone with capacity and desire |

## 🌱 What Mentoring Teaches *You*

Mentoring is not just for the mentee. It's one of the best development tools for the mentor:

```
MENTORING DEVELOPS:
  
  🔭 Curiosity   → Seeing your world through fresh eyes
  👂 Listening   → Hearing what people actually mean
  🗣️ Clarity     → Explaining complex things simply
  🤝 Connection  → Building a network that lasts decades
  💪 Patience    → The foundation of all good management
```

## 🔍 Real Example: Two Mentoring Outcomes

> **Story A — Camille's first job at BigTechCo:**
> Manager showed her to a desk and left her alone. She didn't know how to ask for help, felt like a failure, and eventually left for grad school.

> **Story B — Camille's first job after grad school:**
> She was given a mentor who encouraged questions, did pair programming with her, helped her understand unit testing. She was productive within days and learned more in her first few months than in her entire time at BigTechCo.

**Same person. Same skills. Different mentoring = completely different outcome.**

## 💡 Key Takeaways

| # | Takeaway |
|---|---|
| 1 | **Prepare before the intern arrives** — a bad start wastes everyone's time |
| 2 | **Have a project, not just tasks** — interns need something to own |
| 3 | **Listen more than you talk** — it's the #1 mentoring skill |
| 4 | **Calibrate your check-in frequency** — don't set it and forget it |
| 5 | **Mentoring builds your network** — today's intern is tomorrow's colleague |
| 6 | **Don't be an alpha geek** — it creates fear, not respect |
| 7 | **Onboarding docs should be living documents** — new hires improve them |
